ShowTheBill

What should a medical procedure actually cost? Built from public data.

How do we know these numbers?

What this is. Every price here comes from public data — the price files hospitals are now required to post, Medicare's published rates, and hospitals' own Medicare cost reports. We use them to estimate what a procedure should cost and to show how far a hospital's price sits above that. These are estimates for context — not a quote, and not your actual bill.

What we don't do. We never invent a number: if a hospital doesn't post a price, we show nothing rather than guess. The prices are the hospital's facility fee — the room, equipment, and staff — and the doctor's separate reading fee is billed apart and isn't included here. Cost-report data runs a year or two behind.

Your privacy. Anything you type — a bill you're checking, your location — stays in your browser. It's never sent anywhere, and never rides along in a shared link.

Not medical, legal, or financial advice.

Full methodology & sources

Coverage

We cover 66 shoppable codes (64 of the 70 CMS-specified shoppable services, with six documented exclusions: one deleted CPT and five inpatient bundles), plus eight add-on codes — 74 priced codes in all. Fifteen are imaging codes (MRI, CT, ultrasound, X-ray, mammography) that get a full cost build-up; the rest (lab, office visits, procedures, surgery) are shown as posted price vs. Medicare only.

How the estimates are built

For the imaging codes we line up three independent numbers and look for agreement: a bottom-up cost build (labor + equipment + overhead from named assumptions), a cost-to-charge cross-check from each hospital's Medicare cost report, and what Medicare actually pays. The cost-to-charge ratio is department-wide, not procedure-specific — a blunt instrument, used only as a cross-check. The 26 breadth-only Wyoming hospitals show the cost-report cross-check and Medicare reference but no bottom-up build, because their scan volumes aren't publicly reported. The per-component cost inputs (staff time, equipment) are still labeled low-confidence assumptions, not yet sourced figures — most so for CT, ultrasound, X-ray, and mammography.

Medicare references

We compare against Medicare's technical rate — the facility's fee — so it matches the hospital's posted facility charge; the radiologist's separate reading fee is excluded throughout. The exact rate depends on the service: OPPS for most outpatient services, the physician fee schedule (PFS) or lab schedule (CLFS) for the few Medicare doesn't pay under OPPS. A few codes have no comparable Medicare facility rate and are shown without one.

Rural hospitals

Critical Access Hospitals are paid by Medicare on a cost basis (~101% of cost), not standard rates, so for them the Medicare figure shown is a benchmark, not the literal payment. Every Wyoming hospital sits on Medicare's Frontier Wage Floor, which makes its locality-adjusted reference equal the national rate.

The bill checker

The Check-a-bill tool compares what you were charged against that hospital's own posted prices and Medicare — entirely in your browser. When a charge runs far above one code's price, it names the most common add-on (e.g. color Doppler on an ultrasound) and can generate an editable dispute letter pre-filled with the sourced numbers and standard patient-rights requests. The letter is assembled and downloaded entirely in your browser; for any code we have no benchmark for, it asks for an explanation rather than citing a price. It says "commonly," never that your specific bill contains it.

Finding what's near you

Set a location — a hospital, town, or ZIP — and the comparison groups by relevance: in-state hospitals nearest first, with neighboring-state options one click away (flagged to check your plan, since insurance networks follow state lines more than mileage). Distances are straight-line miles, not drive times. Your location and compare list stay in your browser.
